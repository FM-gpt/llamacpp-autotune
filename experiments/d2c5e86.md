# d2c5e86 — speculative decoding (0.5B draft, n_max=8)

**Phase 2 (server harness).** Target Qwen2.5-14B Q4_K_M + draft Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct
Q8_0, both fully on GPU. Greedy (temp 0) → lossless: output is byte-identical to the
no-spec baseline, only speed changes. Measured via `bench_server.py` (server `/v1`
timings, 5 reps).

**Result (validated, with cooldown + clock check):**
- no-spec baseline: 30.03 ± 0.08 tok/s @ 1811 MHz
- **spec n_max=8: 37.32 ± 0.14 tok/s @ 1890 MHz → +24%**, 30% draft acceptance
- reproduced: 37.02 ± 0.26 @ 1888 MHz (second run)
- clock-normalized it's still faster per MHz, so the win is real, not a boost artifact.

**Decision: keep** — biggest single win in the project. Caveat: peak VRAM ~11.2 GB,
over the self-imposed 11 GB budget (fine on the 12 GB card; could reclaim by lowering
server `-c` from 4096 or shrinking the draft KV).

## The n_max curve and a measurement trap (the real lesson)

First sweep, back-to-back with no cooldown, produced a **non-physical curve**: a valley
at n_max=5/6/7 (23–28 tok/s) between peaks at n_max=3 (32) and n_max=8/9 (36–37), and
n_max=5 gave 30.0 then 27.7 on repeat. At temp 0 everything is deterministic, so that
shape is impossible — it was the GPU **power-capping** (170 W limit, ~129 s cumulative
SW power cap) clocking down by variable amounts between runs.

Fix: added an SM-clock sampler to the harness (`avg_sm_clock_mhz`) so every run flags
its own throttle state, and put a cooldown between runs. With that, n_max=8 reproduced
cleanly. **Numbers without a clock reading are not comparable on a power-capped card.**

The directional finding survives: n_max≈8 is the sweet spot; n_max=16 is *slower than
no spec at all* (−22%) because the draft over-speculates and rejected tokens waste
target compute. The fine peak (8 vs 9 vs 10) needs locked clocks to split.
